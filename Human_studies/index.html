Human Kratom Studies

This page summarizes controlled human studies evaluating kratom (Mitragyna speciosa) and its primary alkaloids, mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH).

Included are one FDA-registered clinical research result and peer-reviewed pharmacokinetic investigations examining safety, metabolism, subjective effects, and dose response under controlled laboratory conditions.

FDA-Registered Human Laboratory Study (ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT06072170)

This Phase I clinical study evaluated the safety and subjective effects of single-dose oral kratom powder in healthy adult volunteers. The study was conducted under controlled laboratory conditions.

Study Design

  • Healthy adult participants
  • Opioid-experienced (not opioid-na√Øve)
  • Single-dose administration
  • Five escalating dose levels (up to approximately 12 grams of kratom powder)
  • Placebo-controlled, clinical research setting

Phase I studies are designed to evaluate short-term safety and pharmacologic effects. They are not intended to evaluate long-term use, dependence, or chronic exposure.

Formation of 7-Hydroxymitragynine (7-OH)

The kratom material used in the study contained minimal pre-formed 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH).

However, measurable 7-OH was detected in participants‚Äô blood after dosing. This indicates that 7-OH was produced inside the body through metabolic conversion of mitragynine.

7-OH has stronger activity at the mu-opioid receptor than mitragynine. Blood concentrations of 7-OH increased with higher kratom doses.

The presence of 7-OH in circulation reflects internal metabolic formation rather than high levels in the plant material itself.

Subjective Effects (‚ÄúDrug Liking‚Äù and ‚ÄúFeeling High‚Äù)

Participants completed standardized rating scales measuring subjective drug effects, including ‚Äúdrug liking‚Äù and ‚Äúfeeling high.‚Äù

At higher doses, statistically significant increases in these measures were observed compared to placebo.

  • Effects were dose-dependent.
  • Lower doses produced minimal subjective changes.
  • Higher doses produced measurable increases in reported drug effect.

Reports of intense euphoria were limited, but measurable abuse-liability signals were present at higher dose levels.

Adverse Effects Observed

The most commonly reported treatment-emergent adverse events included:

  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Somnolence (sleepiness)
  • Anxiety
  • Presyncope (near-fainting)

Adverse effects increased in frequency at higher dose levels.

No deaths were reported. No severe respiratory depression was observed in this single-dose setting.

Participant Withdrawals

Some participants were withdrawn from the study:

  • Elevated liver enzymes (ALT/AST) at higher dose levels
  • Gastrointestinal intolerance
  • Vasovagal syncope during blood draw (fainting episode)

Two participants at the highest dose level were withdrawn due to liver enzyme elevations.

No serious adverse events were reported in this study.

Study Conduct and Sponsorship

The study was funded by a commercial kratom manufacturer (Johnson Foods / NP Pharma).

Clinical trial operations were conducted by a contract research organization (SGS Nutrasource, Canada).

Several authors disclosed consulting relationships with the sponsor.

2024 Human Pharmacokinetic Study (Molecules)

This peer-reviewed study evaluated how mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) move through the body after both single and repeated daily doses of dried kratom leaf powder.

Study Purpose

The goal was to measure blood concentrations, metabolism, accumulation over time, and steady-state levels under controlled conditions.

7-Hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) Formation

The kratom powder used in this study contained extremely low amounts of 7-OH prior to administration.

After dosing, measurable 7-OH was detected in participant blood samples. This confirms that 7-OH was primarily produced through liver metabolism of mitragynine rather than being present in high concentrations in the original plant material.

Although 7-OH blood levels were lower than mitragynine levels, 7-OH was consistently detectable across dose levels.

7-OH has greater mu-opioid receptor activity than mitragynine. The study demonstrates that even low-7-OH plant material results in systemic exposure to this metabolite.

Single-Dose Findings

  • Blood levels of mitragynine increased proportionally with dose.
  • 7-OH levels increased alongside mitragynine.
  • No unexpected metabolic byproducts were identified.

Higher doses resulted in higher peak concentrations (Cmax) and overall drug exposure (AUC).

Repeated Daily Dosing (15 Days)

Participants received repeated daily doses for approximately two weeks.

  • Steady-state concentrations were reached in approximately 7‚Äì9 days.
  • Low-to-moderate accumulation of mitragynine was observed.
  • 7-OH remained detectable throughout repeated dosing.

The ratio of 7-OH to mitragynine was highest after single doses and decreased somewhat with repeated dosing, but did not disappear.

Adverse Events

Reported adverse effects were similar to those seen in the 2026 safety trial:

  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Somnolence
  • Headache

No severe respiratory depression was observed in the controlled study setting.

Regulatory-Relevant Context

This study confirms that:

  • 7-OH is produced systemically after kratom ingestion.
  • 7-OH exposure increases with higher doses.
  • Repeated dosing results in sustained exposure over time.

The study does not evaluate long-term chronic use, dependence development, or polydrug use scenarios.

Funding Disclosure

The study was funded by Johnson Foods. Authors disclosed consulting relationships with the sponsor.

Summary Across Controlled Human Studies

Controlled human studies of kratom remain limited in number, but the studies summarized here provide structured information on metabolism, short-term tolerability, and measurable subjective effects under supervised conditions.

Key Findings (Plain Language)

  • 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) is produced in the body. Even when the kratom product contains very little 7-OH, participants still develop measurable 7-OH in blood, indicating metabolic conversion from mitragynine.
  • Higher doses increase exposure. Both mitragynine and 7-OH blood levels rise with dose, and repeated dosing results in sustained exposure.
  • Drug-effect ratings increase at higher doses. In the FDA-registered single-dose study, higher doses were associated with statistically significant increases in subjective ratings such as ‚Äúdrug liking‚Äù and ‚Äúfeeling high.‚Äù
  • Short-term side effects were common and dose-related. Nausea, dizziness, headache, sleepiness, and similar effects were reported more often at higher doses.
  • Some participants were withdrawn. Withdrawals occurred for reasons including elevated liver enzymes at higher doses and gastrointestinal intolerance.

What These Studies Do Not Evaluate

  • Long-term, high-frequency chronic use over months or years
  • Adolescents and pediatric exposures
  • Population-level incidence (how common events are in the general public)
  • Real-world retail variability, including concentrated extracts and polydrug use
  • Development of dependence and withdrawal under long-term daily use

Controlled trials are designed to measure effects under specific conditions. Surveillance systems (mortality, poison control, adverse event reporting) capture broader real-world patterns and are presented elsewhere on this site.
